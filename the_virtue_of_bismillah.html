The Virtue Of Bismillah#

Reading Objectives

By the end of this reading, the reader should be able to:

  • Understand the spiritual meaning and significance of “Bismillah” (In the Name of God).

  • Explain the central metaphor of the desert journey and its moral lesson.

  • Recognize how dependence on God transforms human weakness into strength.

  • Identify how all creation symbolically acts “in the Name of God.”

  • Reflect on the concepts of remembrance, gratitude, and reflection as the “price” for divine bounties.

  • Apply the principle of acting in God’s name to daily life.

Beginning with God’s Name#

In the name of God, the merciful, the compassionate And from Him do we seek help.

All praise be to God, the Sustainer of All the Worlds, and blessings and peace be upon our master Muhammad, and on all his Family and Companions.

Bismillah, “In the Name of God,” is the start of all things good. We too shall start with it. Know, O my soul! Just as this blessed phrase is a mark of Islam, so too it is constantly recited by all beings through their tongues of disposition.

Explanation

The text opens by grounding all action, praise, and help-seeking in God. “Bismillah” is presented not merely as a phrase, but as a universal principle embedded in existence itself.

Quick Check!

  1. Why does the author begin with praise and blessings?

  2. What does it mean that “Bismillah” is a mark of Islam?

  3. How can beings “recite” something without speech?

The Desert Journey: A Parable of Dependence#

If you want to know what an inexhaustible strength, what an unending source of bounty is Bismillah, listen to the following story which is in the form of a comparison. It goes like this:

Someone who makes a journey through the deserts of Arabia has to travel in the name of a tribal chief and enter under his protection, for in this way he may be saved from the assaults of bandits and secure his needs. On his own he will perish in the face of innumerable enemies and needs.

Explanation

The desert symbolizes a dangerous world where survival depends on protection beyond oneself. The “name” represents recognized authority and protection.

Quick Check!

  1. Why is the desert an effective symbol for life?

  2. What does traveling “in the name of a tribal chief” provide?

  3. What happens to one who travels alone?

Humility Versus Pride#

And so, two men went on such a journey and entered the desert. One of them was modest and humble, the other proud and conceited.

The humble man assumed the name of a tribal chief, while the proud man did not. The first travelled safely wherever he went. If he encountered bandits, he said: “I am travelling in the name of such-and-such tribal leader,” and they did not molest him. If he came to some tents, he was treated respectfully due to the name. But the proud man suffered indescribable calamities throughout his journey. He both trembled before everything and begged from everything. He was abased and became an object of scorn.

Explanation

Humility opens the door to protection and honor, while pride isolates a person and exposes them to fear and humiliation.

Quick Check!

  1. How does humility function as protection?

  2. What consequences does pride bring in the story?

  3. What inner qualities are being contrasted?

The Soul Addressed: Life as a Desert#

My proud soul! You are the traveler, and this world is a desert. Your impotence and poverty have no limit, and your enemies and needs are endless. Since it is thus, take the name of the Pre-Eternal Ruler and Post- Eternal Lord of the desert and be saved from begging before the whole universe and trembling before every event.

Explanation

The parable is personalized. The reader is reminded of their vulnerability and urged to seek divine protection rather than self-reliance.

Quick Check!

  1. Why does the author address the soul directly?

  2. What do “impotence” and “poverty” symbolize?

  3. How does God’s name transform the journey?

Weakness Turned into Strength#

Yes, this phrase is a treasury so blessed that your infinite impotence and poverty bind you to an infinite power and mercy; it makes your impotence and poverty a most acceptable intercessor at the Court of One All-Powerful and Compassionate.

The person who acts saying, “In the Name of God,” resembles someone who enrolls in the army. He acts in the name of the government; he has fear of no one; he speaks, performs every matter, and withstands everything in the name of the law and the name of the government.

Explanation

Human weakness becomes a means of connection to divine power. Acting in God’s name grants authority, courage, and stability.

Quick Check!

  1. Why is weakness described as “acceptable intercession”? 2.How does the army metaphor clarify the concept?

  2. What changes when actions are done in God’s name?

All Creation Acts in God’s Name#

At the beginning we said that all beings say “In the Name of God“ through the tongue of disposition. Is that so?

Indeed, it is so. If you were to see that a single person had come and had driven all the inhabitants of a town to a place by force and compelled them to work, you would be certain that he had not acted in his own name and through his own power, but was a soldier, acting in the name of the government and relying on the power of the king.

In the same way, all things act in the name of Almighty God, for minute things like seeds and grains bear huge trees on their heads; they raise loads like mountains. That means all trees say: “In the Name of God“ fill their hands from the treasury of mercy, and offer them to us. All gardens say: “In the Name of God,” and become cauldrons from the kitchens of Divine power in which are cooked numerous varieties of different foods. All blessed animals like cows, camels, sheep, and goats, say: “In the Name of God,” and produce springs of milk from the abundance of mercy, offering us a most delicate and pure food like the water of life in the name of the Provider.

Explanation

Nature’s power far exceeds its apparent capacity, indicating reliance on divine command rather than inherent strength

Quick Check!

  1. How does nature “act” in God’s name?

  2. Why are seeds and grains emphasized?

  3. What conclusion does the author draw from this?

Nature’s Obedience and Divine Command#

The roots and rootlets, soft as silk, of plants, trees, and grasses say: “In the Name of God,” and pierce and pass through hard rock and earth.

Mentioning the name of God, the name of the Most Merciful, everything becomes subjected to them. The roots spreading through hard rock and earth and producing fruits as easily as the branches spread through the air and produce fruits, and the delicate green leaves retaining their moisture for months in the face of extreme heat, deal a slap in the mouths of Naturalists and jab a finger in their blind eyes, saying:

“Even heat and hardness, in which you most trust, are under a command. For like the Staff of Moses, each of those silken rootlets conform to the command of, And We said, O Moses, strike the rock with your staff (Al-Baqarah 2:60) and split the rock. And the delicate leaves fine as cigarette paper recite the verse “O fire,” We ordered, “Be cool and peaceful for Abraham!” (Al-Anbiya 21:69) against the heat of the fire, each like the members of Abraham, peace and blessings be upon him.

Explanation

Natural laws themselves submit to divine will, refuting claims that matter alone governs reality.

Quick Check!

  1. How do roots challenge material explanations?

  2. What message is conveyed to naturalists?

  3. Why are Qur’anic examples included?

Giving and Taking in God’s Name#

Since all things say: “In the Name of God,” and bearing God’s bounties in God’s name, give them to us, we too should say: “In the Name of God.” We should give in the name of God, and take in the name of God. And we should not take from heedless people who neglect to give in God’s name.

Explanation

Human behavior should mirror the divine order—acknowledging God in exchange, generosity, and consumption

  1. What does it mean to give and take in God’s name?

  2. Why is heedlessness dangerous?

  3. How does this shape ethical living?

The True Price of Blessings#

Question: We give a price to people, who are like tray-bearers. So what price does God want, Who is the true owner?

The Answer: Yes, the price the True Bestower of Bounties wants in return for those valuable bounties and goods is three things:

one is remembrance, another is thanks, and the other is reflection. Saying, “In the Name of God“ at the start is remembrance, and, “All praise be to God” at the end is thanks. And perceiving and thinking of those bounties, which are priceless wonders of art, being miracles of power of the Unique and Eternally Besought One and gifts of His mercy, is reflection.

However foolish it is to kiss the foot of a lowly man who conveys to you the precious gift of a king and not to recognize the gift’s owner, it is a thousand times more foolish to praise and love the apparent source of bounties and forget the True Bestower of Bounties.

Explanation

God asks for no material compensation, only awareness, gratitude, and contemplation.

Quick Check!

  1. What are the three “prices” for blessings?

  2. How is each practiced in daily life?

  3. Why is ignoring the true giver considered foolish?

Conclusion: Living by God’s Name#

O my soul! If you do not wish to be foolish in that way, give in God’s name, take in God’s name, begin in God’s name, and act in God’s name. And that’s the matter in a nutshell!

Explanation

The text concludes with a concise call to conscious, God-centered living.

Glossary#

Word / Phrase

Definition

Abased

Humiliated; made to feel small or worthless.

Abundance

A very large amount; plenty.

Apparent

Seeming to be true on the surface but not necessarily the full reality.

Authority

Power or the right to command and be obeyed.

Bearing (bounties)

Carrying or delivering gifts.

Bestower

One who gives something generously.

Bismillah

An Arabic phrase meaning “In the Name of God.”

Bounties

Gifts or blessings, especially generous ones.

Cauldrons

Large pots used for cooking; here used metaphorically.

Calamities

Serious misfortunes or disasters.

Compassionate

Deeply caring about the suffering of others and wanting to help.

Conceited

Having too much pride in oneself.

Court (of God)

A symbolic way to describe God’s presence or judgment, like a royal court.

Disposition

A natural tendency or built-in way of behaving.

Enroll

To officially join something, such as the army.

Eternally Besought One

One who is always depended upon or prayed to.

Heedless

Not paying attention; careless or unaware.

Impotence

Lack of power or ability.

In a nutshell

Expressed briefly and clearly.

Inexhaustible

Impossible to use up; endless.

Intercessor

Someone or something that helps connect one party to another; a mediator.

Materialism / Naturalism

The belief that only physical matter exists and explains everything.

Merciful

Showing compassion and forgiveness.

Naturalists

People who believe nature alone explains all events, without divine involvement.

Parable

A short story that teaches a moral or spiritual lesson.

Price (spiritual sense)

What is asked in return, not money but actions or attitudes.

Provider

One who supplies what is needed.

Reflection

Careful thinking; deep consideration of meaning or purpose.

Remembrance

Actively keeping something in mind; in this text, remembering God.

Rootlets

Very small roots of plants.

Scorn

Strong disrespect or contempt.

Subjected

Brought under control or authority.

Sustainer

One who supports, maintains, or provides for something continuously.

Tongue of disposition

A metaphor meaning expressing something through natural behavior, not speech.

Treasury

A place where valuable things are stored; here, a metaphor for spiritual wealth.

Unending

Having no limit or end.

Withstand

To resist or survive difficulty.
